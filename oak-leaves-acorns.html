Two gifts fall from the cork oak each year, and the whole montado is built around catching them. Leaves, which feed the soil; and acorns, which feed the animals — and, through them, the people.

The leaves come down and become litter, then humus: the slow decay that returns carbon, nitrogen and phosphorus to the ground, fed on by the actinomycetes and Bryobacter of the acid soil. A montado fertilises itself, in part, with its own fallen leaves.

The acorns fall into the montanheira — the autumn season when the black Iberian pigs are turned loose beneath the oaks to fatten on them. From those acorns comes the montado's most famous product: acorn-fed bellota ham, the taste of the woodland made flesh.

This is the deal at the heart of the montado: the oak gives leaf and acorn; the soil and the herd give it back as fertility and as a living. A thousand-year-old economy, still running on what falls from a tree.

What the cork and holm oaks give this land. Fallen leaves become litter and humus, recycling nutrients through the soil; acorns feed the Iberian black pigs of the montanheira, giving rise to acorn-cured ham — the montado's living economy, falling from the trees.

The montado is an agro-silvo-pastoral system — trees, soil and grazing woven into one — and the oaks' leaves and acorns are the thread. Leaves feed the soil and its microbes; acorns feed the animals; the animals feed the people and, with their dung, the ground.
One tree, feeding the soil below it and the herd around it — and asking, in return, only to be kept.

Leaf litter is a primary route by which carbon and nutrients return to montado soil, decomposed by fungi, actinomycetes and acidobacteria into the humus that holds the ground's fertility and water.

To sit under a cork oak in autumn — leaves turning to soil, acorns dropping to the pigs — is to watch a whole culture fall from a tree, and be gathered up again. This is the montado at its most human, and most whole.

The oaks' fallen leaves become the humus that feeds their own soil. Each autumn brings the montanheira, the acorn-foraging season that fattens pigs into bellota ham — the montado's signature — in an agro-silvo-pastoral system where trees, soil and grazing work as one.

The oak is the montado’s carbon sink made visible. Its long-lived wood, regenerating cork and deep soils hold carbon for centuries, which is why cork-oak woodlands are valued as Mediterranean carbon stores. Whether that store grows or empties turns on the tree’s survival: a stand lost to Phytophthora stops fixing CO₂ and begins releasing it, flipping the montado from sink to source across the carbon cycle — the point where one tree’s fate meets the global atmosphere.
The cork and holm oaks are the montado's centre. Everything here flows toward them — carbon from the air, nitrogen and phosphorus freed by microbes, and the pathogen that threatens their roots.

Carbon dioxide

  • Its leaves fix CO₂ into wood, cork and acorns
  • Stored for centuries as a carbon sink
  • The montado takes in more than it releases

Glucose

  • Photosynthesis turns that carbon into sugar
  • The oak builds itself from it
  • And leaks a share to feed its root microbes

Ammonia

  • Every protein in the oak traces back to ammonia
  • Fixed from the air by the pasture's legumes
  • Fed to the tree through litter and soil

Dihydrogen phosphate

  • The phosphorus its roots take up
  • Freed from locked minerals by soil microbes
  • Scarce in the montado's acid ground

Phytophthora

  • The water mould that rots its fine roots
  • Cause of the montado's a seca decline
  • Held back by the soil's defender microbes

Streptomyces

  • Its fallen leaves are broken into humus
  • By actinomycetes and acidobacteria
  • Returning carbon and nutrients to the soil
Two things fall from the oak each year: the leaf, which becomes the soil, and the acorn, which becomes the pig, which becomes the ham, which becomes a table, a village, a way of life. A thousand years of people have lived on what drops from these trees in the quiet of an Alentejo autumn.
