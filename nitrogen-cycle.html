The montado is scarcely fertilised, and yet it has stayed fertile for centuries. The secret is not a substance but a loop — a slow circle in which nitrogen is borrowed from the sky, passed hand to hand through the living, and returned.

It begins with fixation: bacteria in the roots of clovers and brooms break the air's N₂ into ammonia. Plants take it up and build protein; animals eat the plants; their dung and the falling leaves return the nitrogen to the soil, where other microbes pass it along.

Then it can leave again. Some is lost to streams, some breathed back to the sky by denitrifying bacteria — including as nitrous oxide, a powerful greenhouse gas. A healthy montado keeps these leaks small: it holds its nitrogen, and reuses it.

Humans have doubled the nitrogen entering the world's cycle, and much of it now pollutes water and air. The montado is a working memory of the other way — a landscape that runs, almost, on a closed loop.

the closed loop

The Nitrogen Cycle · The hidden loop that keeps poor soil from running dry
Nitrogen moves in a cycle — fixed from the air, taken up by plants, passed through animals, returned to the soil, and eventually released again. In the near-unfertilised montado this loop is what keeps the ground fertile; globally, humans have overloaded it.
A biogeochemical cycle — the path an element traces through living things (bio), the earth (geo) and its chemistry, round and round.
Almost every organism in this Field Guide has a place on this wheel: the rhizobia that fix, the plants that take up, the actinomycetes and Bryobacter that break down litter and return it. The nitrogen cycle is the montado's circulatory system.
Four turns of the wheel — borrow, build, return, release.

Human activity — synthetic fertiliser, fossil fuels, industrial livestock — has roughly doubled the reactive nitrogen entering the global cycle, with consequences from algal blooms to greenhouse gases. Low-input systems like the montado point the other way.

Fertility, seen this way, is not a stock you spend but a circle you keep turning. The montado's genius is how little it lets escape — a closed loop dressed as a wood-pasture.

In four steps — fix, take up, return, release — the cycle keeps nitrogen moving. Humans have roughly doubled the reactive nitrogen entering the biosphere, and where the loop leaks it escapes as N₂O, a potent greenhouse gas. A traditional montado runs on almost none of that synthetic nitrogen.

Borrowed from the sky, built into a leaf, eaten, dropped, and broken down, handed back to the soil, and lent to the next spring. Nothing is bought here. Nothing, if the montado is well, is wasted.
