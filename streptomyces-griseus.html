A fallen leaf is unmade into soil and medicine alike — the scent that rises after the first rain is that labour, made air.

In 1943, in a spoonful of manured field soil in New Jersey, a graduate student named Albert Schatz found a grey, thread-like bacterium making a molecule that would change the world. They called the molecule streptomycin — the first antibiotic to cure tuberculosis, the first medicine ever drawn from the soil's own chemistry.

The bacterium was Streptomyces griseus — strepto-, twisted; -myces, fungus; griseus, grey. It grows like a mould, branching into fine filaments and grey spore-chains, but it is a bacterium, and its genus turned out to be the soil's pharmacy: most of the antibiotics in medicine were first found in these grey threads underground.

The same chemistry that heals people can defend a tree. In the ground, Streptomyces lays down chitinases and glucanases — enzymes that dissolve the cell walls of fungi and of Phytophthora — alongside antibiotics that stop the pathogen outright. A strain of S. griseus cut root-rot death by nearly half in trials.

To meet a Streptomyces is to smell it first: geosmin, the scent of rain on dry earth after a drought, is a molecule these bacteria make. In the montado it is the smell of a soil that is mature, alive and whole — the ground the oaks depend on, quietly manufacturing both the cure and the defence.

the soil's pharmacy

Streptomyces griseus · The soil’s pharmacy
The grey, filamentous bacterium that gave the world streptomycin — its first cure for tuberculosis. In the montado soil the same genus works as a pharmacy against a seca, dissolving the walls of Phytophthora with chitinases and glucanases and stopping it with antibiotics.
Streptomyces — Greek streptos, "twisted," + mykes, "fungus": it looks like a mould but is a bacterium.
griseus — Latin, "grey," for its grey spore masses.
Actinobacteria — Streptomyces among them — are a sign of a mature, healthy soil. In cork-oak ground they are counted among the bacteria that hold plant pathogens in check, working by antibiosis and by the enzymes that dissolve a fungal or oomycete wall. What defends the montado, in the end, is the same chemistry that fills a pharmacy.
A single strain of S. griseus can make dozens of different bioactive compounds — a whole dispensary in one cell.

Against Phytophthora, the actinobacteria attack the wall. Chitinases and β-1,3-glucanases break down the fibres that hold the water mould's hyphae together, while secreted antibiotics stop its growth; strain H7602 destroyed Phytophthora hyphae outright and reduced root-rot mortality by about 47% in trials.

The lesson is old and simple. The first cure for tuberculosis was not invented — it was found, already being made in the dark by a bacterium in a spoonful of soil. The montado's defence against a seca may be there too, if the ground is kept alive long enough to hold it.

In 1943, streptomycin was isolated from S. griseus — the first antibiotic ever to cure tuberculosis. The genus has since yielded 32 distinct structural classes of bioactive compounds, and most of the antibiotics in clinical use today were first found in Streptomyces. In the soil that same chemistry turns protective: one strain, H7602, cut Phytophthora root-rot mortality by nearly 47%.

Streptomyces runs the montado’s ‘return’ step. By breaking down the toughest lignin and litter, it decides how fast stored carbon goes back to the air rather than staying locked in soil — and that balance is what makes the woodland a net carbon sink or not. Warming speeds the decay, so this soil microbe sits inside a global feedback: the faster it works, the more carbon returns to the carbon cycle and the atmosphere. It also guards the oak against Phytophthora, keeping the sink alive.
Streptomyces griseus is the soil's pharmacy and one of its great recyclers — breaking down the toughest plant matter and making antibiotics that hold pathogens in check.
