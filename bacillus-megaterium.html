At the oak’s root a giant dissolves the stone, and phosphorus long locked in mineral becomes leaf and acorn.

To find the bacteria that keep the montado alive, researchers went to the driest ground they could — three Portuguese cork-oak forests, from humid to semi-arid, and dug up the living soil beneath the oaks. From more than seven thousand colonies, a few dozen carried every trait a tree could want. The best of them came from the driest forest of all.

And the one that stood out was the biggest. Bacillus megaterium — "the great beast" — is among the largest bacteria known, a rod up to four thousandths of a millimetre long, a hundred times the bulk of the E. coli in your gut. Almost a cell you could see. In 1884 Anton de Bary, who first named it, simply called it Grosstier: the big animal.

It does not fight the water mould. It feeds the tree. Cork oak grows in poor, acid soil where phosphorus is locked in stone; the great beast pours out organic acids that dissolve it free, scavenges iron with siderophores, and reshapes the root itself — more side roots, more root hairs, a denser net to drink from.

Like its smaller cousin it folds a spore and waits out the summer, already at home in the heat and drought. It is not a medicine sprayed onto the montado from outside. It is the montado — a fertiliser the ground grows for itself, if only we leave the soil alive enough to grow it.

the great beast

Bacillus megaterium · The largest grain in dry ground
One of the largest bacteria in the soil, Bacillus megaterium was singled out from the driest Portuguese cork-oak forest as its most capable growth-promoter — unlocking phosphorus, scavenging iron, and reshaping the oak's roots. Not a weapon against a seca, but a way to keep the tree strong enough to resist it.
Bacillus — Latin, "little rod."
megaterium — "great beast," from the Greek therion; de Bary nicknamed it the big animal in 1884. A little rod that is a great beast.
The montado's soils are thin, acid and poor. What lets an oak stand for two hundred years in such ground is not richness but partnership — a rhizosphere of bacteria that pry nutrients loose and hand them to the root. In a survey of cork-oak forest soils, spore-forming Bacillus was the one genus found in every forest, and B. megaterium from the driest of them carried the fullest set of growth-promoting traits.
Four gifts to the tree — none of them an attack. Where Pseudomonas and Bacillus subtilis wound the pathogen, the great beast simply makes the oak harder to kill.

Inoculated onto roots, B. megaterium suppresses the primary root and multiplies the lateral roots and root hairs — a larger surface to drink and feed through, built by chemical signals rather than by any nutrient the bacterium itself supplies.

Because these isolates are already native to cork-oak ground — bred by the same drought the oaks endure — they point to a home-grown restoration: not importing a cure, but returning the montado's own microbes to soils that intensive management has stripped bare.

At about 4 µm long — roughly a hundred times the volume of E. coli — this is one of the giants of the bacterial world, at home anywhere between 3 and 45 °C, including the montado's heat. Tellingly, it was the driest cork-oak soils that yielded the most capable plant-growth promoters: 51 of 323 isolates carried the full PGPR trait set.

Not every ally carries a sword. The great beast brings no poison to the fight — only a mouthful of dissolved stone, a thread of iron, a wider root to drink the winter rain. Strength is its own defence. Feed the tree, and the tree will stand.
