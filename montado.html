The montado is a cultivated oak woodland in the Alentejo region of southern Portugal — cork and holm oaks spaced across open pasture, combined with grazing, cork harvesting, and crops. Neither wild forest nor conventional farmland, it is an agro-silvo-pastoral system shaped over centuries.

Today the system is in decline. The oaks are dying faster than they regenerate, and much of why lies in the soil: a microbial community that governs whether trees establish, how nutrients cycle, and whether pathogens such as Phytophthora cinnamomi take hold. The soil microbiome is the montado's foundation — and the part of it hardest to see.

The Microscopic Montado brings this invisible layer into view. Through an interactive field guide of the bacteria, molecules, and plants that sustain the system, alongside maps of the montado's changing extent and a conversation with those who work the land, it treats soil microbiology not as a technical detail but as the basis of the landscape's survival.

It is made, first, for the people who work this land — such as Eva, whose farm is one of my two field sites and is now threatened by a solar project routed across it — so that the value beneath their feet becomes something they can see and argue for. It is made with local partners already defending the montado (Alexander von Hildebrand's Alentejo Comvida in Montemor; the ADPM — Association for the Defence and Preservation of the Montado), and for a wider public asked to re-value a landscape whose most vital part is microscopic.

Built on existing research: cork-oak decline and soil-microbiome studies at the University of Évora, and the Vale Formoso Erosion Experimental Centre in Mértola (established 1961), one of Europe's oldest soil-erosion stations.

Site 1 · Herdade de Montalvo — a montado
A working cork-oak montado in the Alentejo (EN253, km 14.7). What was pressing on the community here was not the farm itself, but a new mining project planned nearby — one the neighbours were, notably, not resisting. It is a reminder that the montado's future is often decided above the ground, through land-use choices like mining, energy and intensive agriculture, even as much of what this landscape is worth lies unseen, in the soil below.
Site 2 · Eva's montado — near Ferreira do Alentejo
An unnamed holm-oak montado at 38°01′40″N, 8°04′33″W, run by Eva: 200 hectares of oaks, open pasture and a flock of sheep, worked entirely on the rain that falls each year — no irrigation. Eva farms it as one living system, watching how long the sheep graze a patch, because once they strip it bare the soil stops holding its water. Around her, extensive montado is being replaced by super-intensive irrigated olive orchards — heavy soil disturbance, native trees cleared, plantations that exhaust themselves in 15–25 years — and a planned solar project would run power lines across her land. The montado quietly gives water retention, biodiversity and carbon storage, yet its keepers are still barely paid for that value. This is the ground the project speaks from.
