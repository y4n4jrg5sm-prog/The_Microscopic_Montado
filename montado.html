The montado is a cultivated oak woodland in the Alentejo, in southern Portugal — cork and holm oaks spaced widely across open pasture, with sheep and Iberian black pigs grazing beneath them. Neither wild forest nor conventional farmland, it is an agro-silvo-pastoral system, shaped by people over centuries, and it only exists because it is worked. Its biodiversity depends on that human use rather than surviving in spite of it.

Two pressures are closing on it at once, and they are usually discussed apart. Above ground, the extensive montado is being cleared and replaced by super-intensive irrigated olive orchards — plantations that exhaust themselves within twenty or twenty-five years, and arrive described as modernisation. Below ground, the oaks are dying faster than they regenerate, their roots rotted by a soil pathogen, Phytophthora cinnamomi, until a centuries-old tree withers over a few seasons.

One of these problems belongs to agricultural economics and the other to forest pathology. This project sits on the seam between them — and the seam is made of soil. Whether a young oak establishes, whether this acidic ground stays fertile without fertiliser, and whether the pathogen spreads or is held back, is decided by a community of microorganisms too small to see. That community is the structure holding the landscape up, and the part of it with almost no public representation at all.

The harm here is slow. There is no felling, nobody standing over a dying tree — only a subsidy, a tractor pass, a compacted soil, and a pathogen that reaches a root fifteen years later. A landscape can be lost like that without anyone deciding to lose it. The Microscopic Montado is an attempt to make that slow loss legible: a field guide of the bacteria, molecules and cycles that sustain the system, and a network that binds them to the global scale. In a landscape damaged this quietly, choosing how to show the soil is not a description of the problem — it is a way of intervening in it.

It is made, first, for the people who already read this ground — the farmer whose montado became one of my two field sites, and the agronomists and cooperatives around them — extending their reading one layer further down, into the biology that decides whether the surface they know stays alive. As it stands the platform only carries this knowledge outward; it has not yet been built with anyone, and taking it back to the ground it is about is the work that comes next.

Site 1 · Herdade de Montalvo — a montado
A working cork-oak montado in the Alentejo (EN253, km 14.7). What was pressing on the community here was not the farm itself, but a new mining project planned nearby — one the neighbours were, notably, not resisting. It is a reminder that the montado's future is often decided above the ground, through land-use choices like mining, energy and intensive agriculture, even as much of what this landscape is worth lies unseen, in the soil below.
Site 2 · A holm-oak montado — near Ferreira do Alentejo
A holm-oak montado at 38°01′40″N, 8°04′33″W, run by an organic producer who describes herself as an ecosystem farmer: 200 hectares of oaks, open pasture and a flock of sheep, worked entirely on the rain that falls each year — no irrigation. She farms it as one living system, watching how long the sheep graze a patch, because once they strip it bare the soil stops holding its water. Around her, extensive montado is being replaced by super-intensive irrigated olive orchards — heavy soil disturbance, native trees cleared, plantations that exhaust themselves in 15–25 years — and a planned solar project would run power lines across her land. The montado quietly gives water retention, biodiversity and carbon storage, yet its keepers are still barely paid for that value. This is the ground the project speaks from.
A super-intensive olive plantation — what replaces the montado
This is what moves in when the montado goes: a super-intensive, drip-irrigated olive orchard, the trees packed into dense hedgerows on ground that has been cleared, ploughed and laid bare. Yields are high but brief — plantations like this exhaust themselves in roughly 15–25 years before they must be grubbed out and replanted. Where the oaks held water, carbon and biodiversity in the soil for centuries, the olive monoculture draws them down in decades.
