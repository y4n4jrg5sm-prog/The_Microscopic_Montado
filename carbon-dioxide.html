The montado breathes. By day, ten thousand cork-oak crowns pull CO₂ from the air and pack its carbon into leaf, wood, cork and acorn. By night, and as everything decays, some of it is breathed back out. In, and out, like a slow lung.

Carbon dioxide is where every living thing here begins. A single molecule — one carbon, two oxygens — caught out of the sky by a leaf and turned, with sunlight, into sugar. Everything green in the montado is, quite literally, air made solid.

But the balance is not even. Cork oaks are long-lived and slow, their deep soils hold carbon for centuries, and the montado takes in more than it gives back — a working carbon sink in a warming Mediterranean.

The same gas that feeds the forest is now, in excess, unsettling its climate: hotter summers, deeper droughts, and more of the disease that kills the oaks. The montado both draws down carbon and stands in the path of what carbon is doing to the world.

the breath of the wood

Carbon Dioxide (CO₂) · The forest breathing in and out
The gas every leaf begins with. Cork oaks fix atmospheric CO₂ into wood, cork and acorns, and the montado's long-lived trees and deep soils make it a net carbon sink — even as rising CO₂ drives the heat and drought that threaten the oaks.
Carbon — from Latin carbo, "coal, ember."
CO₂ — one carbon between two oxygens; the air's carbon, and the raw material of photosynthesis.
Photosynthesis is the montado's foundation: leaves take in CO₂ and, with water and light, build sugar — the first link in a chain that ends in cork, acorns and the flesh of the animals. Respiration and decay send some carbon back; what stays is the montado's slow, deep carbon store.
A single gas, doing two opposite things to the montado at once.

Cork-oak woodlands store carbon in wood, cork, roots and deep soil, and are counted among the Mediterranean's important carbon sinks and providers of climate-regulating ecosystem services.

To protect a montado is, in a small way, to protect the climate — and to grasp that the same molecule feeding the forest, in excess, is what threatens it. Care here runs in both directions at once.

Every leaf is built from the carbon in CO₂, and across a life that can exceed two hundred years a cork oak stores a great deal of it. The montado as a whole absorbs more than it emits — a net sink — with much of that carbon held in its deep soil.

The wood breathes in the sky and turns it into bark. One carbon, two oxygens, caught by a leaf, made solid, held for two hundred years. The air that feeds the oak is the same air, too much of it, that is drying the oak to death.
