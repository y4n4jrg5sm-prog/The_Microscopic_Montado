There is phosphorus in the montado's soil — enough, in principle, to grow everything. But much of it is a prisoner. In acid ground, phosphate reaches for aluminium and iron and sets, hard, into minerals the roots cannot touch.

One of those minerals is variscite, a pale green aluminium phosphate, AlPO₄·2H₂O. It is beautiful, and it is a locked door: the phosphorus inside it is real, present, and almost entirely unavailable to a plant.

The montado's soils are exactly the kind that lock it — acid, sandy, poor. Which is why the whole system leans so heavily on the microbes that can pick the lock: bacteria like Bacillus megaterium, which pour out organic acids that dissolve the mineral and set the phosphorus free.

So a stone holds the key to a forest. Phosphorus, more than almost anything, decides how much can grow here — and whether it stays locked in variscite or flows into an oak depends on the smallest, most easily-overlooked lives in the ground.

the locked stone

磷铝石（AlPO₄·2H₂O） · 被锁进石头的养分
In the montado's acid soil, phosphorus binds with aluminium into insoluble minerals like variscite (AlPO₄·2H₂O), beyond the reach of roots. Only phosphate-solubilising microbes can release it again — making them, not the mineral, the true source of the montado's phosphorus.
Variscite — named after Variscia, the old name for the Vogtland region of Germany where it was described.
AlPO₄·2H₂O — hydrated aluminium phosphate.
Phosphorus is the montado's tightest bottleneck. In acid, sandy ground the phosphate ion is quickly fixed by aluminium and iron into minerals such as variscite, so that total phosphorus can be plentiful while available phosphorus stays scarce. What a plant gets depends on what the rhizosphere can unlock.
Why the montado's phosphorus lives and dies by its microbes.

Phosphate-solubilising bacteria release organic acids — gluconic, citric, oxalic — that chelate the aluminium and iron and free the phosphate into solution, where roots can finally reach it.
