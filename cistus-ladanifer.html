01 — Above ground

A shrub nobody grazes, everybody uses

Across the Alentejo it is common knowledge that esteva appears where grazing has failed. Yet the same plant secretes labdanum — a sticky, sweet resin of terpenes and flavonoids exuded by its leaves — which the perfume industry prizes as a fixative that holds a scent in place. The British cosmetics maker Lush is among the buyers sourcing it from Alentejo farmers.

02 — Fire & policy

Resin, fire, and a subsidy rule

That resin is also why esteva burns. In fire-prone country its oils turn the shrub into tinder — but the same fire is part of its life cycle: its hard-coated seeds are cued to germinate by the heat of a passing burn.

There is a bureaucratic edge to this too. Under EU support rules, plots carrying too much scrub can lose eligibility for funding, so farmers clear the estevas off — a quiet collision between fire ecology and agricultural policy.

"It's a species that appears when the montado gets a bit wild and the pasture isn't good."

03 — Below ground

The interesting montado is microbial

Below the resin and the flowers, esteva is doing quiet chemistry with bacteria. It is not a legume; it fixes no nitrogen. Instead it cultivates a lean, specialised rhizosphere. Screens of wild populations recover mostly phosphate-solubilising and siderophore-producing bacteria — organisms that pry nutrients out of poor, acidic soil — and find surprisingly low diversity: a tight crew rather than a crowd.

Two bacteria were first described to science from this very rhizosphere: Bacillus terrae and Delftia rhizosphaerae, both named from the soil around esteva roots in Iberia. The plant is also ectomycorrhizal — and here a bacterium plays matchmaker: Pseudomonas fluorescens acts as a "mycorrhiza helper," improving the fungus's colonisation of the roots. In these acidic Cistus soils the community is dominated by Proteobacteria, Acidobacteria and Actinobacteria.

04 — A real case

The poisoned mines of the pyrite belt

The clearest story sits an hour south of the cork-oak country, in the Iberian Pyrite Belt. At the abandoned São Domingos and Aljustrel mines — soils loaded with copper, lead, zinc and arsenic, baked by 40 °C summers — esteva is one of the few plants that colonises the waste on its own. It behaves as an excluder: it holds the metals down in its roots and rhizosphere instead of passing them up to the leaves.

And that containment is not the plant's work alone. Phytostabilisation of this kind — locking pollutants in place rather than spreading them — depends on the rhizosphere microbiota working alongside the roots. The same lean bacterial crew that helps esteva feed on a poor pasture is, on a ruined mine, helping to hold a landscape's poisons still.

The shrub farmers clear for a subsidy is, underground, doing slow remediation work — with a bacterial crew that overlaps with the rest of this guide.
