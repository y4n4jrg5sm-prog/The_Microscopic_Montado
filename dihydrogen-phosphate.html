A root cannot drink a stone. Before the montado's oaks and grasses can use phosphorus at all, it must arrive in one particular, dissolved shape: the ion H₂PO₄⁻, dihydrogen phosphate.

This is phosphorus made small and mobile — a single phosphate with two hydrogens, drifting in the thin film of water around a root. It is the only form the root's channels are built to take in. Everything else is potential; this is food.

Which form dominates depends on how acid the soil is. In the montado's acid ground, H₂PO₄⁻ is the main player — the very reason the microbes' work of freeing phosphate matters so directly to the tree.

And there is never much of it. The concentration of dissolved phosphate around a root is vanishingly low, drawn down almost as fast as it appears. The montado grows on a trickle — kept flowing, ion by ion, by the life in the soil.

the usable ion

Dihydrogen Phosphate (H₂PO₄⁻) · The only phosphorus a root can drink
The form of phosphorus that roots can actually absorb. H₂PO₄⁻ is dissolved phosphate — scarce, mobile, and dominant in the montado's acid soil. Turning locked mineral phosphorus into this ion is the whole point of the soil's phosphate-solubilising microbes.
Phosphate — from phosphorus, Greek for "light-bearer."
H₂PO₄⁻ — the dihydrogen orthophosphate ion, phosphate's plant-available face.
Plants absorb phosphorus almost solely as orthophosphate ions — mainly H₂PO₄⁻ in acid soils, HPO₄²⁻ in alkaline ones. In the montado's acid ground the balance tips to H₂PO₄⁻, and its supply is the pinch-point of the whole phosphorus story.
From locked rock to living leaf, phosphorus passes through this one narrow gate.

Because dissolved phosphate is so scarce and moves so slowly through soil, roots depend on partners — solubilising bacteria that free it, and mycorrhizal fungi that reach out to fetch it — to keep the trickle flowing.

Everything the montado is — the oaks, the acorns, the flowers, the flesh of the pigs — is assembled, in part, from this one faint ion, kept flowing by the invisible work of the soil.

H₂PO₄⁻ is the form of phosphorus a root can actually take up, and in acid soil like the montado's it dominates over HPO₄²⁻. Yet it exists in the soil solution only at micromolar traces, kept topped up by phosphate-solubilising microbes and fungi.

Not the mineral, not the stone — only this: one small dissolved ion, two hydrogens and a phosphate, adrift in a film of water at the tip of a root. The whole forest waits on so faint a thing.
