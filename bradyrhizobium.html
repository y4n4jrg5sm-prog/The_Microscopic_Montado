For most of its history the montado was never fertilised. Its nitrogen — the element that limits nearly all growth on land — came out of the air, pulled down by bacteria living inside the roots of the plants between the oaks: the brooms, the lupins, the serradella of the old fallows.

The bacterium is Bradyrhizobium, "the slow rhizobium," named for how unhurriedly it grows — a week or more where others take a day. It is the chosen partner of the Genisteae, the broom tribe: Cytisus, Genista, Retama, Lupinus — the yellow-flowered shrubs that colour a montado spring.

Nitrogen gas is 78% of the air and almost inert, its two atoms triple-bonded shut. Almost nothing alive can break that bond — except the enzyme nitrogenase, which the bacterium runs inside a root nodule, shielded from oxygen by a pink pigment, leghaemoglobin, the plant's own cousin of the red in our blood.

The tree-and-pasture system is fed by this quiet chemistry. Clear the brooms, plough the ground, pour on synthetic nitrogen, and the partnership is broken — and a fertility that cost nothing, and ran on sunlight and air, is lost with it.

the slow one

慢生根瘤菌 · 从空气里取氮的共生者
The slow-growing rhizobium that partners the montado's brooms and lupins. Inside their root nodules, Bradyrhizobium runs nitrogenase — the enzyme that pulls nitrogen from the air — feeding the whole agro-silvo-pastoral system without a gram of synthetic fertiliser.
Bradyrhizobium — Greek bradys, "slow," + rhiza, "root," + bios, "life": the slow root-dweller, for its unhurried growth beside the fast Rhizobium.
The montado is an agro-silvo-pastoral system — trees, crops and grazing woven together. Its engine of fertility is the legume: root nodule bacteria and their host plants are, in the words of one study, a powerful tool for improving the montado's pastures. In Mediterranean climates, lupins are the one grain legume that Bradyrhizobium nodulates — and the wild brooms are its constant partners.
A partnership older than agriculture, and still the cheapest fertiliser there is.

Beyond nitrogen, Bradyrhizobium makes siderophores to gather iron and helps its hosts through the montado's dry, iron-poor, acid soils — one reason the brooms can pioneer bare and broken ground.
