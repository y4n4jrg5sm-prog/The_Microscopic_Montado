In the montado the soil is poor in nitrogen, and the pasture between the oaks turns green anyway. Cut into the root of a clover or a lupin growing there and nothing dark runs out; instead you find small swellings along the root, and if you open one it is red inside. The maker of that greenness lies underground, and what it trades is not moisture but air.

The story begins before anyone understood it. For as long as there has been farming, people knew that beans, clovers and lupins left the ground richer than they found it, and sowed them to feed the soil without knowing why. The reason was named late. In 1888 the Dutch microbiologist Martinus Beijerinck drew the cause out of a root nodule and grew it in glass: a bacterium, later called Rhizobium, able to take nitrogen straight from the air. The red was stranger still. It is leghemoglobin, close kin to the pigment that reddens our own blood, made by the plant to hold oxygen away from an enzyme that oxygen would destroy.

The gift runs upward through everything above it. Nitrogen is the element plants can least easily find, though the air is four-fifths nitrogen they cannot use, locked in a bond that human industry can break only with great heat and pressure. In the legumes of the understory — serradella, subterranean clover, wild lupins — Rhizobium breaks it cold, turning unreachable air into the nitrogen that greens the pasture, fattens the stock, and, as it dies back each year, enriches the soil the cork oaks stand in. Across the same agro-silvo-pastoral system of some 3.1 million hectares, this fertilising is done quietly, for free, by cells too small to see.

Its making works from the bottom up. A root hair curls around a single passing cell; the bacterium slips inside and travels down a thread the plant builds to receive it; around it the root swells into a nodule and floods red, shielding the enzyme within. There, in the dark, it pulls that bond apart at the temperature of the soil and hands the plant nitrogen it could never have reached alone. A plant that appears to give nothing away, rooted in poor ground, is feeding a whole woodland out of thin air. This is the other colour beneath the montado: not the black of the ink, but the red of the nodule.

rhizobia

Rhizobia · The montado’s underground nitrogen workers
In the montado, rhizobia do not live as a headline species. They live inside the root nodules of pasture legumes — subterranean clover, serradella, medics and their companions — converting atmospheric nitrogen into ammonia that plants can use. The exchange is simple and profound: the plant pays in sugars, the bacterium pays in nitrogen.
Rhizo- means root. The name points to where the bargain happens: not in the leaf, not in the acorn, but in small pink rooms underground.
Montado soils are often thin, acidic, rain-fed and low-input. The system cannot depend on constant synthetic nitrogen without changing its economics and ecology. Legumes solve this in a slower way: they invite rhizobia into nodules, and the fixed nitrogen later feeds the pasture, the grasses, the grazing animals, and the oaks through litter and soil turnover.

This is why improved Portuguese biodiverse pastures are designed to be rich in legumes: the seed mix often includes species such as Trifolium subterraneum, Ornithopus spp. and annual medics, with rhizobial inoculation supplying the bacterial partner.

Rhizobia are microscopic, but their effect is landscape-sized. Under the cork oaks, legume roots make nodules that turn atmospheric nitrogen into plant-available forms; after grazing and decomposition, that nitrogen re-enters the montado as litter, dung, roots and humus.

The same symbiosis can be read at two scales: pink nodules on a single root, and legume-rich pastures repeated across the Alentejo. For this page, the map matters because rhizobia are not just a lab organism; they are part of a managed landscape.

Montado practice

Legume-rich pastures are a soil technology.

Portuguese sown biodiverse pastures combine many species and varieties, often with more than half legume cover early on. The rhizobial partner is part of the design, not an afterthought.

Dehesa / Montado

Subterranean clover belongs to the same landscape logic.

In southwest Iberian dehesa systems, subterranean clover is treated as a key pasture legume; seed is often inoculated with Rhizobium and helped with phosphorus during establishment.

Research frontier

Scientists still try to move this power into cereals.

Current nitrogen-fixation research treats the legume-rhizobium nodule as the model: a natural factory that can meet crop nitrogen demand, and a prototype for future engineering.

A root curls. A bacterium enters. Sugar moves downward, nitrogen moves up. The pasture is green because something microscopic made a room inside a root.
