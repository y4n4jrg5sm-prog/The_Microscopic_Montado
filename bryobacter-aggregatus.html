In a damp, mossy corner the silent multitude works on, turning moss and rain, grain by grain, into acid soil.

Take a single gram of montado soil and read the DNA in it, and a huge share belongs to a group of bacteria almost no one has ever managed to grow: the Acidobacteria. In acidic forest soil they can be a third or more of everything alive — and yet, decades after their discovery, only a handful of species have been coaxed into a dish.

Bryobacter aggregatus is one of the few. It was first pulled from acidic peat — bryo-, for the moss it lived among; aggregatus, for the way its cells clump into little clusters. Slow, aerobic, acid-loving, it lives on the sugars released as moss and leaf litter slowly decay.

It fixes no nitrogen. It makes no famous drug. It kills no pathogen. Its importance is quieter, and easy to miss: bacteria like it thrive where soil is acidic, low in nutrients, stable and undisturbed — exactly the poor, acid ground the cork oaks grow in — and they fade when the land is ploughed, limed and fertilised.

To find them in abundance is to know the soil is old, quiet and whole. They are the dark matter of the montado: most of what is there, and almost none of it understood. A reminder that the ground we are trying to save is mostly unknown to us — and worth saving anyway.

the dark matter

Bryobacter aggregatus · The silent majority of acid soils
One of the very few cultured members of the Acidobacteria — a phylum that can make up a third of the bacteria in acidic forest soil, yet is almost entirely unknown. Slow, acid-loving and oligotrophic, Bryobacter is a marker of the stable, undisturbed ground the montado depends on.
Bryobacter — Greek bryon, "moss," for the peat and moss it was first found in.
aggregatus — Latin, "clustered," for the irregular cell aggregates it forms.
Cork oak grows on poor, acid, sandy soil — and acid forest soils are the domain of the Acidobacteria, one of the most abundant and diverse phyla in the earth beneath us, between roughly a twentieth and a half of all the bacteria present. Almost none of them will grow in a laboratory. Bryobacter is one of the rare exceptions — a first, faint light on a vast dark continent.
Not a hero, but a witness — the kind of organism whose mere abundance tells you the soil is being left to be itself.

Bryobacter grows on the sugars, uronic acids and heteropolysaccharides freed as plant litter and moss break down — a recycler at the slow end of the carbon cycle, tolerant of acid from pH 4.5 to 7.2, patient where richer, faster bacteria cannot persist.

The uncultured majorityOne of very few named cells in a vast, unknown phylum.
Perhaps the deepest lesson of the montado's soil is humility. We have named a few defenders, a few nitrogen-fixers — and around them lies an unseen majority we cannot yet grow, name or fully understand. Conservation here is not managing what we know, but protecting what we don't.

Acidobacteria can make up anywhere from 5 to 50 percent of a soil's bacteria, yet they are famously hard to grow: this is the only cultured species named in its entire genus, and the first ever grown from its acidobacterial subdivision. It tolerates a pH of 4.5 to 7.2, most at home in the mildly acid ground the montado provides.

Most of what lives here has no name. It will not grow in glass. It makes no medicine, fixes no air, kills nothing, saves nothing we can measure. It only means: the ground is old, and acid, and left alone. We are asked to protect a darkness we cannot yet read.
