In Portugal the disease has a name: a seca, "the drought." Cork oaks yellow from the crown down, shed their leaves, and whole stands fall in rows across one or two summers, as if scorched dry by the sun. The real killer lies underground, and what it needs is moisture.

The story begins before the oaks. In 1838, Portugal recorded a strange disease in its chestnut trees: cut into the roots of a sick tree and a black fluid seeped out, like ink. People called it doença da tinta, ink disease. The black was the colour of tannins oxidising in the wounded root. The pathogen was not native. It likely arose in the New Guinea region, an oomycete more closely related to algae than to fungi, carried across the world in nursery stock and infested soil, able to infect close to a thousand plant species.

More than a century later, it changed hosts. In the 1980s, the dehesas and montados of Spain and Portugal began to die back in patches. In the early 1990s the plant pathologist Clive Brasier isolated the cause from the roots of dying oaks: Phytophthora cinnamomi. Today it is regarded as the leading factor in Iberian oak decline, reaching across an agro-silvo-pastoral system of roughly 3.1 million hectares in southwestern Europe. In the worst-hit areas, cork yield losses have ranged from 40 to 89 percent.

Its killing works from the bottom up. When the soil is warm and wet, dormant spores wake, swim to the finest feeder roots, kill them, then spread up into the larger roots until they choke the tree's conducting system, and the leaves dry from the crown down. A tree standing in mud, its roots eaten away, dies at last in the name of drought. This is a seca.

a seca

Dieback · the montado's invisible epidemic
Since the 1980s, cork and holm oaks across 3.1 million hectares of Iberian montado have thinned, browned and died — a syndrome the Portuguese call a seca. The cause is not in the canopy but in the soil: a microscopic water mould, Phytophthora cinnamomi, rotting the fine roots where no one looks.
Phytophthora — from the Greek, “the plant destroyer.”
cinnamomi — for the cinnamon (Cinnamomum) on which R. D. Rands first described it, Sumatra, 1922.
Crowns thin, leaves yellow, and whole trees can die within a single season — once recorded simply as sudden death. But by the time the canopy shows it, the feeder roots are already gone. The decline is oldest and heaviest in the Alentejo, where the research site sits.
In warm, moist soil the pathogen needs only days to complete a turn — and each turn multiplies the inoculum.

It grows between about 9 °C and 30 °C, fastest near 27 °C — so the cycle races through a mild, wet Mediterranean spring. No film of water in the soil, no swimming zoospores. The disease is confined to the moist. It is heterothallic: sex needs two mating types. Where only one is present, as across much of Iberia, it persists and spreads clonally — by chlamydospores and swimming zoospores.

The canopy is the last place the disease appears. A tree can wilt and die in a single dry spell only because, underground, a girdling collar lesion and the loss of its fine roots have already cut off its water.
The pathogen and the drought are not two problems but one loop. As extremes sharpen — see Global Change — the loop turns faster.

With its fine roots destroyed, a drought-stressed oak can no longer draw water. It dies where it stands.

The same rhizosphere the pathogen invades is home to bacteria that fight it. In suppressive soils, communities of Pseudomonas, Bacillus and Streptomyces hold Phytophthora in check — the montado's microbial immune system, and the organisms you met in the Field Guide.

Pseudomonas

Antibiosis · Iron warfare
  • DAPG & phenazines — antifungal antibiotics
  • Siderophores lock up iron, starving the pathogen
  • Volatiles — 2-undecanone, HCN, ammonia

One strain deformed the pathogen's mycelium and ruptured its membranes.

Bacillus

Membrane attack · Endurance
  • Lipopeptides — iturin, surfactin, fengycin
  • Punch holes in the oomycete's membranes
  • Spore-formers outlast drought beside it

By volatiles alone, one strain cut the pathogen's growth by 76%.

Streptomyces

The soil's pharmacy
  • Antifungal secondary metabolites
  • Cell-wall enzymes — chitinases, glucanases
  • Filamentous actinobacteria of healthy soil

The classic antibiotic-makers — much of medicine's soil pharmacy began here.
