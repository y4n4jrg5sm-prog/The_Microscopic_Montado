Some montado soils kill the oaks. Others, only metres away, do not — the same trees, the same rain, the same buried killer, and yet the seedlings live. Plant pathologists have a plain word for this second kind of ground: suppressive soil.

The suppression is alive. Sterilise the soil and it forgets how to protect; add back a spoonful of the living earth and the protection returns. Somewhere in that spoonful is a bacterium that fluoresces. Under an ultraviolet lamp a plate of it glows a cold, chemical green — the light of a molecule called pyoverdine, a hook the cell throws out to catch iron.

Iron is the currency of the rhizosphere, and there is never enough of it. Pseudomonas fluorescens hoards it — pyoverdine binds iron so tightly that Phytophthora is left hungry at the root. The same cell secretes 2,4-diacetylphloroglucinol and phenazines, small antibiotics that deform the water mould's hyphae, and hydrogen cyanide that drifts through the soil air.

It does not only attack. Colonising the root surface, it whispers to the tree — priming the oak's own immune system so that, when the pathogen arrives, the defences are already awake. Antibiosis, competition, volatiles, induced resistance: four quiet trades, run by a bacterium too small to see, that decide whether a stand of Quercus suber stands or falls.

the green light

Pseudomonas fluorescens · The night-watch of disease-suppressive soil
Wherever a montado soil resists a seca, a fluorescent bacterium is usually part of the reason. Pseudomonas fluorescens starves the pathogen of iron, poisons its hyphae with small antibiotics, and primes the oak's own immunity — the montado's microbial first line of defence.
Pseudomonas — Greek, "false unit," an old word for a germ.
fluorescens — for the green pigment pyoverdine it pours out to scavenge iron, glowing under ultraviolet light.
The montado is not only oaks. Beneath every tree lies a thin, crowded film of life — the rhizosphere — where roots trade sugars for protection, and billions of bacteria compete for a living. Into this film the water mould sends its swimming spores. Whether they reach the root alive depends on who else is already there.
Four ways one bacterium holds a pathogen back. None of them needs a spray, a tractor, or a single euro of subsidy — only a living soil.

The fluorescent pseudomonads are the most studied biocontrol bacteria on Earth; whole natural disease-suppressions of wheat and tobacco have been traced back to the DAPG they make. In the montado, strains antagonistic to P. cinnamomi are being read as a model for the same defence.

Not a cure, but a balance. Suppression is what a whole, unbroken soil community does for free — and what deep ploughing, fungicide and bare intensive ground take away. To protect the oaks may mean, first, protecting the invisible ones that protect them.

More than fifty species are now folded into the P. fluorescens complex, most at home at 25–30 °C — the montado's mild, wet season. It holds pathogens back in four independent ways, chief among them DAPG, the antibiotic behind entire natural disease-suppressive soils.

Pseudomonas fluorescens does two montado jobs at once: it frees phosphate from soil minerals and it shields the oak from Phytophthora with antibiotics and siderophores. Both feed the same large-scale outcome — a productive woodland that keeps its carbon and needs little fertiliser. A single rhizosphere bacterium sits at the junction of the phosphorus and carbon stories, and its work shows up at landscape and global scale.

A tree dies from the roots, in the dark, unseen. So too it is defended — in the dark, unseen, by a cell that carries its own small lamp and spends its life hoarding iron so the killer goes hungry. The montado we can photograph rests on a montado we cannot.
